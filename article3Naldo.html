Key Terms

Lack of Empathy: The inability or unwillingness to understand and share the feelings of others, leading to widespread injustice, suffering, and societal harm.

Massacres and Genocides: Acts of large-scale violence targeting specific groups, often fueled by prejudice, hatred, and lack of empathy, resulting in the unjust deaths of millions throughout history.

The Holocaust: A genocide during World War II in which Nazi Germany, under Adolf Hitler, orchestrated the systematic murder of six million Jewish people, demonstrating the extreme consequences of hatred and lack of empathy.

The Kantō Massacre: A massacre in Japan following the 1923 Great Kantō Earthquake, where rumors and racial prejudices led to the killing of thousands of Koreans by Japanese citizen militias.

Climate Inaction: The failure of world leaders to take meaningful steps toward reducing carbon emissions and addressing climate change, despite agreements such as the Paris Treaty, often due to selfish interests and lack of empathy.

Paris Treaty (Paris Agreement): A global agreement signed in 2015 aimed at reducing carbon emissions and combating climate change, largely undermined by world leaders' inaction and broken promises.

The Kantō Massacre: How Fear and Prejudice Fueled a Tragic Injustice

The Kantō massacre was the result of social stigmas similar to those in Nazi Germany. In Kantō, a densely populated area of the Japanese capital of Tokyo, a large earthquake of 7.9 occurred. The earthquake would trigger a series of events that would raise tensions between Koreans and Japanese in Tokyo. Soon rumors would spread about the Korean population, who would take advantage of the chaos to steal, burn, kill Japanese and even stage a coup d'état.
